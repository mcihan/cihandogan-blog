4. The Recurring Mistakes

4.1 The "and then" addiction (1,210 occurrences)

You use "and then" as a sentence-starter, a clause-glue, and a pause-filler — the way Turkish uses sonra. In English, this turns a paragraph of distinct events into one run-on stream. C1 speakers rotate: so, after that, next, meanwhile, eventually, as a result, then, or just a full stop and a new sentence.

What you said What you should say Why
"And then he heard. And then my CTO said that, okay, we will change some structures." (L10) "He heard. My CTO then said we would change some structures." A new event usually starts with the subject, not "and then".
"And then they create PR. And then they calls. All of the childs." (L10) "They create a PR. Next, they call all the children." A new action deserves a new sentence — not another "and then".
"checks. And then you give that. And then product owner getting this information. And then you can see…" (L10) "Checks. Then you hand it over. The product owner picks it up — at that point you can see…" Three different connectives keep the listener oriented.
"And then. And then some of this. And then they said." (L10) (delete — start the sentence) When you say "and then" three times in a row, you're stalling, not adding information. A small uh is more honest.
"And then bill, etc." (D's lesson but the pattern is the same) "Then the bill, and so on." Drop the "and"; the next item already has it.

Your worst stretch was lesson 10: 71 uses in 1,752 words = 4.05 per 100 words. Listen back to it once with a tally and you'll hear it immediately.
