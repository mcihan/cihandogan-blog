The "and then" run-on chain (your #1 issue)

You said (Lesson 17, tech talk)
"Pace. So they like it and then there's the issue on the recording. They record our presentation as well in this meeting because in the meeting there was 60 people or something, but rest of them couldn't see it because we did it on just one location and then just some people come in that day. So because of it they record it and then they share it entire the company today, recorded but there is a problem in the audio... and then we will go to Manchester and..."
Should be
"They liked the talk. But there was an issue with the recording: only about 60 people could attend in person, so they recorded it to share company-wide. The audio came out broken, though. As a result, my manager suggested we redo it in Manchester instead."
Why: Break the chain. One idea per sentence. Use varied connectors: so · as a result · though · instead.

6 · Spotlight: The "and then" Monster

Why this is your #1 priority

Across 24 lessons, you said "and then" 1,123 times — that's once every 51 words, or roughly once every 12 seconds of speech. Even worse: 270 sentences contain "and then…and then" (back-to-back run-ons within a single utterance). Your single biggest monologue (Lesson 17) hit 122 and-thens in one session.

What this does to a native listener:

  • They lose the timeline. Every "and then" feels like a new event, even when it isn't.
  • They can't tell the main point from the side detail. Everything is on the same conveyor belt.
  • They wait for the punchline that never lands. The chain just keeps going.

What to do instead, in priority order:

SituationReplace "and then" with
The next thing happened". After that, …"
This caused that". So, …" or "As a result, …"
You're adding a related detail", which means…" or "— and"
You're contrasting"But…" or "Although…"
You're moving on to a new topic(Full stop. New sentence.)
You need a pause to think(Silence) — don't fill it with "and then"

Target for next 12 lessons: Drop "and then" usage from ~47/lesson to under 10/lesson.
