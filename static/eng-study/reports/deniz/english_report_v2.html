5 · Real Sentences from Your Lessons — What You Said vs What You Should Say

The "like" filler problem (your #1 issue)

You said (article discussion, pubs)
"I agree with the article. I think nowadays like people are more aware of the like the even in the UK there is a drinking culture, people are more conscious about the health issues so they try to avoid drinking basically. It makes sense."
Should be
"I agree with the article. Nowadays, people are more aware — even in the UK there's still a drinking culture, but people are more conscious about health, so they try to drink less. It makes sense."
Why: Three "likes" and one "basically" deleted. The sentence becomes confident, not hedging. like as a filler signals uncertainty even when you're sure of what you're saying.
You said (weather)
"Yesterday it was like disaster like it rains like four times."
Should be
"Yesterday was a disaster — it rained four times."
Why: Three "likes" in one short sentence. Also: "it rained" (past) and "a disaster" (article).

"Basically" — your second filler

You said (multiple lessons)

Why: "Basically" should mean "in essence / fundamentally". You're using it as a verbal placeholder. Drop it entirely 4 times out of 5.

Why: Six "yeahs" do not equal one "right" or "exactly". You said "yeah yeah yeah" 627 times across 43 lessons. Native listeners read this as anxiety, not enthusiasm.

3rd-person singular -s (Turkish carry-over)

You said (about your friend's surgery)
"They will remove throat. And after that, she doesn't have any more throat. And she will get pill. It's called hormone. And this pill helps body to produce T4 and T3."
Should be
"They'll remove her thyroid. After that, she won't have a thyroid anymore. She'll have to take a pill called a hormone. This pill helps the body produce T4 and T3."
Why: The organ is the thyroid (not throat). Articles: "a pill", "the body". And drop "and" at the start of every sentence — it's a Turkish habit.
You said (about your husband)
"He erased all of his special life. When he come back from work to home, the same, he doesn't remember the work."
Should be
"He completely separates work from his personal life. When he comes home, he doesn't think about work."
Why: "He comes" (3rd-person -s). "Come back home" — no to. "Erased" is too strong; "separates" is what you mean. "Special life" is a Turkish calque (özel hayat) — in English it's "personal life" or "private life".

"This kind of things" (plural mismatch)

You said (5+ different lessons)
"They are making profit basically from this kind of things... I heard this kind of things because of this alcohol effect... Maybe this kind of things also affects... In the pub this kind of things it's free."
Should be
"They make their profit from things like this... I've heard that sort of thing happens because of alcohol... That kind of thing affects it too... In a pub, that kind of thing is free."
Why: Either this kind of thing (singular) or these kinds of things (plural). "This kind of things" is grammatically impossible — singular determiner + plural noun. Most natural: things like this or that sort of thing.

Double comparatives (Turkish "daha daha")

You said (gift culture)
"If you bring alcohol to someone's house, like a bottle of alcohol, it's a really expensive gift basically and they become more happier happier. They become happier."
Should be
"If you bring a bottle of alcohol to someone's house, it's an expensive gift, so they're much happier when you do."
Why: Happier already means "more happy". You can't say "more happier". For short adjectives (1–2 syllables), use just the -er form: happier, better, bigger, faster, kinder. For long ones, use more: more interesting, more important. Never both.
You said (Korea reference)

Should be
"Even Turkey is much better."
Why: "Better" already contains the comparison. Drop "more".

Why: Home works as an adverb after motion verbs — no to needed. Same family: go upstairs, go downtown, go abroad.

6 · Spotlight: The "Like" Habit

Why this is your #1 priority

Across 43 lessons, you said "like" 2,122 times — that's once every 35 words, or roughly twice every sentence. For comparison, a native British speaker uses "like" as a filler about once every 150–200 words.

"Like" has four valid meanings in English:

Valid useExample
1. To enjoy something"I like coffee."
2. Similar to / such as"Cities like London…"
3. Reported speech (American)"She was like, 'no way'."
4. Approximation"It cost like £20."

Every other use is filler. What you actually do:

  • Before nouns: "I drink like green tea" → "I drink green tea"
  • Between clauses: "I went there like and saw…" → "I went there and saw…"
  • As a softener: "it's like really nice" → "it's really nice"
  • Stalling for words: "she's like… you know… really…" → silence + thought

Drill: Pick three sentences from your last lesson. Read them aloud. Every time you hit a "like" that isn't one of the four valid uses, snap your fingers and re-say the sentence without it.

Target for next 10 lessons: Reduce "like" from ~49/lesson to under 20/lesson.
